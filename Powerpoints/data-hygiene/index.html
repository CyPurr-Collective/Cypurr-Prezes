Threat Modelling

  • What do I have?
  • What is it worth?
  • What can go wrong?
  • How likely are adverse events?
  • Who are possible adverse actors?
  • Who are our allies?
  • If the worst happens, what are the consequences?
  • Are you willing to accept said consequences?

Open Ports and You!

How to Secure your System(s)

  • Make only necessary ports available (firewall)
  • Make sure said services are not vulnerable to overflow or fuzzing attacks (sanitize all user-submitted data)
  • Ensure services are not vulnerable to denial of service attacks (containers, orchestration, and replicability)
  • Avoid UPnP and other firewall-traversing shenanigans
  • Monitor for and block unusual requests (disable remote ssh, for example)
  • Distrust IoT devices as they generally have poor software support (do you really trust your smart light bulb to get regular security patches?
  • Use hardware-based authentication (u2f or 2fa)

 

Source: OWASP

Breaking Passwords

As of 2011, it was possible to test 3 billion passwords per second!

 

That means that a random password 10 characters long can be broken in 1 day!

 

Georgia Tech Research Institute recommends 15 characters at least (July 2018).

Source: GA Tech News

This website is a giant database of leaked and or exposed passwords, stored in their cryptographic form. They do not share the actual passwords, but allow you to safely check your password against a known list.

They have 154 million records, spanning many websites and services.

Password Manager

A password manager stores your strong password

2-FA

2-FA (Two-Factor Authentication) is a way of protecting your accounts and data using two factors.

Most of the time, your 10 digit phone number or one-time code sent to your email address are used as the 2nd factor.

Much better than passwords alone, but still subject to some risk.

U2f

U2F (Universal Two-Factor) replaces the one time code with a cryptographically secure system that you carry with you.

 

It's a literal key to your data.
